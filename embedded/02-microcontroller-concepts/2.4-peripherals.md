# Part 2.4 — Peripherals

A peripheral is the **embedded system's interface to the physical world**. It converts between digital signals (what the CPU understands) and physical phenomena (light, voltage, time, motion, communication). Peripherals are what make a microcontroller an *embedded* system — without them, it's just a small computer that can't sense or act.

This page covers the five categories every modern MCU includes.

---

## 2.4.1 — The Analogy

Think of the MCU's **CPU** as the brain. Peripherals are the **senses and the limbs** that connect the brain to the world.

| Body part | MCU peripheral |
|---|---|
| Eyes (light sensor) | ADC reading from a photoresistor |
| Ears (microphone) | ADC reading from a microphone |
| Voice (speaker) | DAC driving an audio output |
| Fingers (touching, switching) | GPIO reading buttons / driving LEDs |
| Internal sense of time | Timer / Counter peripherals |
| Heartbeat-controlled muscle | PWM driving a motor |
| Mouth + ears for talking | Serial peripherals (UART, SPI, I²C) |

Without senses and limbs, the brain can think but can't *do* anything. Same for the MCU.

---

## 2.4.2 — GPIO (General Purpose Input/Output)

**GPIO pins** can be configured as either digital inputs or digital outputs. "General purpose" means the function isn't hardwired — software decides on each pin.

| Mode | What it does | Common uses |
|---|---|---|
| **Input** | Reads a digital level (high `1` or low `0`) | Button press, switch state, sensor digital output |
| **Output** | Drives a pin to high or low | LED on/off, motor enable, relay control |

A GPIO pin can only be in one of **two states** — there is no "in between" (that's what ADCs and PWM are for).

GPIO is the most flexible peripheral. With clever timing, you can also **bit-bang** other protocols (UART, SPI) on GPIO pins, though dedicated peripherals are more efficient.

---

## 2.4.3 — Timers and Counters

Both built on the same hardware (an internal counter register), differentiated by **what they count**:

| Peripheral | What it counts | Example use |
|---|---|---|
| **Timer** | Clock ticks (time) | Generate a precise 100 ms delay; measure the duration of an event |
| **Counter** | External events / pulses | Count revolutions of a motor encoder; count pulses from a sensor |

Timers and counters are the foundation for many other features:
- **Timeouts** — wait at most X ms for an operation
- **Periodic interrupts** — fire an ISR every 10 ms
- **PWM generation** — see next section
- **Frequency measurement** — count edges over a known interval

Throughout this book, **timers handle precise timekeeping**; **counters track event occurrences**.

---

## 2.4.4 — PWM (Pulse Width Modulation)

PWM emulates an **analog signal of varying intensity** by rapidly toggling a digital output at a fixed frequency, varying the **duty cycle** (percentage of time spent high).

```
   100% duty cycle  ─────────────────────────────  (full power)

    75% duty cycle  ▓▓▓▓▓▓░░░▓▓▓▓▓▓░░░▓▓▓▓▓▓░░░  (3/4 power)

    50% duty cycle  ▓▓▓░░░▓▓▓░░░▓▓▓░░░▓▓▓░░░▓▓▓  (half power)

    25% duty cycle  ▓░░░░░▓░░░░░▓░░░░░▓░░░░░▓░░░  (quarter power)

     0% duty cycle  ─────────────────────────────  (no power)
                    │── period ──│
```

The **average power** delivered to the load is proportional to the duty cycle. Common applications:
- **Motor speed control** — duty cycle = motor speed
- **LED brightness (dimming)** — duty cycle = brightness perceived
- **Servo positioning** — pulse width selects servo angle

Cheap and efficient — no analog circuitry needed, just a digital output toggled fast enough that the load's inertia averages it out.

## 2.4.5 — ADCs and DACs

The bridge between the **digital world** (CPU, registers, code) and the **analog world** (continuous voltages, sensor outputs).

| Peripheral | Direction | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| **ADC** (Analog-to-Digital Converter) | Analog → Digital | Samples a voltage and produces a digital value |
| **DAC** (Digital-to-Analog Converter) | Digital → Analog | Takes a digital value and outputs a corresponding voltage |

### ADC details

An ADC has three properties that matter:
- **Resolution** — how many bits in the output (e.g. 12 bits → values 0 to 4095)
- **Sample rate** — how many samples per second (Hz)
- **Reference voltage** — the maximum voltage the ADC can read

Common ADC sources: temperature sensors (thermistor), light sensors (photoresistor), microphones, joysticks, battery voltage monitoring, IMU outputs (some).

### DAC details

DACs are less common in MCUs (PWM often substitutes). When present, used for:
- Audio output (waveform generation, sound synthesis)
- Driving analog circuits that need a smooth voltage
- Function generators (test signals)

Many MCUs (including the ESP32-C3) **have ADCs but no DAC** — PWM fills the role for most needs.

## 2.4.6 — Serial Communications (UART, SPI, I²C)

Serial = **bit-by-bit** data transfer over a small number of wires (vs parallel, where all bits travel together over many wires). Cheap pin count, longer wires possible. Three protocols dominate embedded:

| Protocol | Wires | Speed | Multi-device | Typical use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **UART** (Universal Async Receiver/Transmitter) | 2 (TX, RX) — async | Low to medium | No (point-to-point) | Debug console, host PC link |
| **SPI** (Serial Peripheral Interface) | 4 (MOSI, MISO, SCK, CS) — sync | High (10s of MHz) | Yes (one CS per device) | Sensors, displays, SD cards |
| **I²C** (Inter-Integrated Circuit) | 2 (SDA, SCL) — sync | Low to medium | Yes (addressed devices) | Sensors, EEPROMs, low-pin-count peripherals |

### Why three different protocols?

Each makes a different trade-off:

- **UART** is the simplest. No clock line; both sides agree on baud rate ahead of time. Good for debug output and host PC communication. Limited to two devices on a wire.
- **SPI** is fast and full-duplex (data flows in both directions at once), but needs a chip-select line per device. Cheap on bandwidth, expensive on pins for many devices.
- **I²C** uses just two wires for any number of devices (each addressed). Slower than SPI, but pin-efficient. Ideal for hooking up many small sensors.

You'll see all three on most MCUs, including the ESP32-C3.

---

## 2.4.7 — Quick Reference

| Peripheral | Purpose | Key trait |
|---|---|---|
| **GPIO** | Digital input or output | Two states (high / low), software-configurable |
| **Timer** | Measure / generate time intervals | Counts clock ticks |
| **Counter** | Count external events | Counts pulses on a pin |
| **PWM** | Emulate analog via duty cycle | Cheap, no analog circuitry |
| **ADC** | Read analog voltages | Resolution + sample rate matter |
| **DAC** | Output analog voltages | Less common, often replaced by PWM |
| **UART** | Serial async, 2 wires, point-to-point | Debug console |
| **SPI** | Serial sync, 4 wires, fast | Multi-device, pin-heavy |
| **I²C** | Serial sync, 2 wires, addressed | Multi-device, pin-light |
