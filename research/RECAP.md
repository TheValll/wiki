## 01 — [How to Read a Paper](./01-reading-papers.md)

| § | Concept | What / for what | Intuition |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1.1 | Why three passes (not one) | Read at three depths, deciding after each whether to go deeper | Don't pay full price up-front; budget reading by ROI |
| 1.2 | First pass: bird's-eye | Title → abstract → intro → conclusions; ~5-10 min triage | "Should I keep reading at all?" |
| 1.3 | Second pass: grasp the content | Skim figures, tables, key equations, related work; ~1 hour | Understand what was done, not how |
| 1.4 | Third pass: virtual re-implementation | Re-derive results, challenge assumptions; several hours | Could you reproduce or extend this? |
| 1.5 | Literature survey | Pass-1 sweep over conferences → pass-2 the relevant → pass-3 the foundational | The same triage, applied to a whole field |
| 1.6 | Connecting to the PhD path | Map shortlist → identify lab clusters → start a reading log | Use papers to map labs and PIs, not just topics |

## 02 — [How to Write a Great Research Paper](./02-writing-papers.md)

| § | Concept | What / for what | Intuition |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2.1 | Start writing early | Begin the paper before the result is finished | Writing reveals what you don't yet know |
| 2.2 | Identify your key idea | Boil the contribution down to one sentence | If you can't say it in a sentence, you haven't found it yet |
| 2.3 | Tell a story | Problem → struggle → resolution structure | A paper is a narrative, not a log |
| 2.4 | Nail the introduction (one page) | Hook + context + contribution list, on page 1 | The reader decides to continue from this page alone |
| 2.5 | Put related work at the end | Don't bore the reader with citations before the idea | Build trust first, situate in the field after |
| 2.6 | Lead the reader by the hand | Forward references, examples, signposts | Never let the reader wonder "where am I going?" |
| 2.7 | Listen | Take feedback seriously; readers are always right about confusion | If they're confused, the writing is at fault |
