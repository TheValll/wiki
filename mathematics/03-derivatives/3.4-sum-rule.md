# 3.4 — Sum Rule

**What it does:**
The derivative of a sum is the sum of the derivatives. Lets you split a complicated expression into independent terms and differentiate each one separately.

Imagine a group of people walking on **parallel treadmills**, each at their own speed. The total distance of the group grows at a rate equal to the sum of the individual speeds — you can track each person independently and add the results. The sum rule says differentiation is linear: no cross-talk between terms.

## Intuition — Independent Tracks, No Cross-Talk

When you add two functions, each one lives its own life. They don't interact. The total rate of change is just the sum of the individual rates.

**The physical image.** Two people jogging on **parallel treadmills**. Each runs at their own pace. Their belts don't touch. The total distance the pair has covered grows at a rate equal to the sum of the two individual speeds — nothing more, nothing less.

```
      Person A: 6 km/h                Person B: 4 km/h
      ═══════════════>                ═══════════════>

      Combined distance grows at 6 + 4 = 10 km/h
```

### Why it works

When `f(x) = u(x) + v(x)` and you push `x` by a tiny `dx`:

- `u` changes by an amount proportional to its own slope
- `v` changes by an amount proportional to its own slope
- `f` changes by the sum of the two

No cross-terms. No interaction. That's because adding is a **linear** operation — the two terms sit side by side without multiplying, dividing, or feeding into each other.

### The same logic applies to subtraction

`(u − v)' = u' − v'`. One treadmill speeds you up, the other pulls you back. The net rate is the difference.

### It extends to any number of terms

With three, four, or a hundred terms, each one differentiates on its own and gets added up at the end. This is why differentiating a polynomial is easy: you handle each power independently and sum.

**What to remember.** Differentiation is **linear**: sums break apart, each term differentiates by itself, nothing crosses over. If a function looks like `u + v + w + …`, the derivative is `u' + v' + w' + …`, full stop.

## Formula

```
(u + v)' = u' + v'
(u − v)' = u' − v'
```

## Simple example

`f(x) = x² + 3x`
```
f'(x) = 2x + 3
```

## Complex example (polynomial + trig + log)

`f(x) = x³ − 4x² + cos(x) − ln(x)`
```
f'(x) = 3x² − 8x − sin(x) − 1/x
```
Each term is differentiated in isolation, then summed.
