# 1.9 — Characteristic Equation:  det(A − λI) = 0

**What it does:**
Finds the **eigenvalues** `λ` of a matrix `A`. Eigenvalues are the scalar amounts by which eigenvectors are stretched — the "natural frequencies" of a transformation. Core of PCA, mechanical vibration analysis, moments of inertia, and robot stability.

Imagine the **rubber sheet** again. Most arrows on the sheet get rotated *and* stretched by `A`. But a few special arrows (eigenvectors) only get **stretched** — they keep pointing in the same direction. The stretching factor for each such arrow is an **eigenvalue**. To find them, we ask: "for what `λ` does `A` behave as a pure scaling by `λ`?" That happens exactly when `A − λI` crushes its eigenvector to zero, i.e. when `det(A − λI) = 0`.

## Intuition — Finding the Machine's Natural Axes

A matrix reshapes space: most arrows get rotated **and** stretched. But a few **special arrows** are only stretched (or shrunk, or flipped) — their direction is left unchanged, the machine only rescales them. Those special arrows are called **eigenvectors**, and the scale factor each one receives is its **eigenvalue** `λ`. The characteristic equation `det(A − λI) = 0` is the tool that finds every such `λ` at once.

**The physical image.** Picture the rubber sheet under machine A and draw a lot of arrows on it. Turn the machine on, watch what happens:
- Most arrows come out **tilted** — direction changed.
- For a few very specific directions, the machine only **elongates, shrinks, or reverses** the arrow along its own line — direction unchanged, length changed.

Those direction-stable lines are the **natural axes** of the machine. If A is a horizontal stretch by 3, the x-axis is a natural axis (arrows along x come out longer, still along x) and the y-axis is another (arrows along y are left untouched, scale factor 1). Every matrix has its own private set of natural axes, one eigenvalue per axis.

---

### From "natural axis" to `det(A − λI) = 0`

We are searching for directions `v` (not zero) such that A just scales them by some factor `λ`:

> *"A sends v to λ times v"*

Rearranging this sentence — pulling everything to one side — gives:

> *"the machine `A − λI` sends v to the origin"*

Here `A − λI` is the machine A with `λ` subtracted from its diagonal (intuitively: "A, weakened by a uniform shrink of factor λ along every axis"). The sentence now says: **`A − λI` takes a non-zero arrow and crushes it to zero**.

But only a machine that has **destroyed a dimension** can send a non-zero input to the origin. By §1.6, a machine destroys a dimension exactly when its determinant is zero. So:
```
(A − λI) has a non-zero vector in its kernel   ⇔   det(A − λI) = 0
```

That is the characteristic equation. Solving it is asking: *for which values of λ does the adjusted machine collapse a dimension?* The answers are the eigenvalues — the only scale factors for which a direction-stable axis can exist.

---

### Why eigenvalues and eigenvectors matter everywhere

- **Robotics — principal axes of inertia.** A rigid body's inertia tensor has natural axes: spin the body around one and it rotates cleanly, with no wobble. The eigenvalues are the principal moments of inertia; the eigenvectors are the principal axes.
- **Machine learning — PCA.** Given data with complicated correlations, PCA finds the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix — the directions along which the data spreads the most. The eigenvalues tell you how much spread each natural direction carries.
- **Graphics and physics — stable modes.** Vibrating strings, rotating planets, modes of a resonant cavity — all are eigen-problems. Eigenvectors are the shapes that do not mix under the dynamics; eigenvalues tell you how each shape grows, decays, or oscillates.

Whenever a system has natural modes that evolve independently of each other, eigenvalues and eigenvectors are the right language.

**What to remember.** The characteristic equation `det(A − λI) = 0` is **the search for scale factors λ that make `A − λI` collapse a dimension** — equivalently, the search for the direction-stable axes of machine A. Eigenvectors are those natural axes; eigenvalues are how much the machine stretches (or flips, or shrinks) along each of them. Nearly every *"what are the natural modes of this system?"* question in physics, robotics, and ML is a hunt for eigenvalues.

## Formula

```
det(A − λ·I) = 0
```

For a 2×2 matrix `A = [[a, b], [c, d]]`:
```
A − λI = [a − λ    b  ]       det(A − λI) = (a − λ)(d − λ) − b·c
         [  c    d − λ]
```

For a 3×3 matrix `A = [[a, b, c], [d, e, f], [g, h, i]]`:
```
A − λI = [a − λ    b      c  ]
         [  d    e − λ    f  ]
         [  g      h    i − λ]
```

The equation `det(A − λI) = 0` yields the characteristic polynomial in `λ`. Its roots are the eigenvalues.

## Simple example (2×2)

```
A = [4  1]
    [2  3]

A − λI = [4−λ   1 ]
         [ 2   3−λ]

det(A − λI) = (4−λ)(3−λ) − 1·2
            = λ² − 7λ + 12 − 2
            = λ² − 7λ + 10
            = (λ−2)(λ−5) = 0

⇒ λ₁ = 2,  λ₂ = 5
```

## Complex example (3×3 — inertia tensor of a robot link)

```
I = [5  0  0]
    [0  3  1]
    [0  1  3]

I − λ·Id = [5−λ   0    0 ]
           [ 0   3−λ   1 ]
           [ 0    1   3−λ]

det = (5−λ) · [(3−λ)² − 1]
    = (5−λ) · (λ² − 6λ + 8)
    = (5−λ)(λ−2)(λ−4) = 0

⇒ λ₁ = 5,  λ₂ = 2,  λ₃ = 4
```
→ Principal moments of inertia are 2, 4, and 5 — the rotation axes for which torque aligns with angular acceleration.
