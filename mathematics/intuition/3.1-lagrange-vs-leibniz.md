# 3.1 — Lagrange vs Leibniz: Two Accents for One Idea

Pure-intuition companion to §3.1 of [`../03-derivatives.md`](../03-derivatives.md). No formulas, no exercises.

---

A derivative is one concept — "how fast is this thing changing right now?" But two different notations coexist in every textbook, every paper, every lab board. They mean the exact same thing. They emphasize different flavors.

**The physical image.** Imagine a car with two gauges on the dashboard.

### The Lagrange gauge — the speedometer

```
          ┌──────────────────┐
          │                  │
          │        60        │
          │        ▲         │
          │       km/h       │
          │                  │
          └──────────────────┘
```

A single needle reading a single number at this instant. "The derivative at time `t` is 6." Compact, direct, no structure shown. Written `f'(t)`.

### The Leibniz gauge — distance-over-time made visible

```
          ┌──────────────────┐
          │                  │
          │  Δx = 3 m        │
          │  ─────────       │
          │  Δt = 0.05 s     │
          │                  │
          │  →  60 m/s       │
          └──────────────────┘
```

A visible ratio. "A tiny time `dt` produces a tiny distance `dx`. Their ratio is the derivative." Written `df/dt`. The notation *shows you what is being divided by what*.

---

### When each one shines

| Situation | Better notation | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Compact calculation | **Lagrange** `f'(x)` | No clutter — just the answer |
| Physics with units | **Leibniz** `df/dt` | Units jump out: `dx/dt` is visibly m/s |
| Chain rule | **Leibniz** `df/dx = df/du · du/dx` | The fractions visually "cancel" — a free mnemonic |
| Higher derivatives | Both fine | `f''(x)` vs `d²f/dx²` |
| Several variables | **Leibniz** `∂f/∂x, ∂f/∂y` | Shows which variable you vary |

---

### A small warning on `dx`

In Leibniz notation, `dx` is written like a number — but it isn't really one. It's a *placeholder* for "an infinitesimally small change in x". The formal meaning is always a limit. The *visual* fraction just happens to behave as if the pieces were numbers — and that coincidence is what makes the chain rule feel obvious when written in Leibniz.

Treat `df/dx` as a symbol first, a fraction only for intuition and mnemonic tricks.

---

**What to remember.**

Two notations, same idea. **Lagrange `f'`** is the speedometer — one number now. **Leibniz `df/dx`** is the ratio-made-visible — a tiny change in `f` over a tiny change in `x`. Pick whichever makes the situation clearer. Physics and chain rule → Leibniz. Quick arithmetic → Lagrange.
