# 3.8 вҖ” Partial Derivatives: One Direction at a Time

Pure-intuition companion to В§3.8 of [`../03-derivatives.md`](../03-derivatives.md). No formulas, no exercises.

---

When a function depends on several variables, you can't ask "what's the slope?" вҖ” there's no single slope. The graph is now a **landscape**, and the slope depends on which direction you step. A partial derivative answers that question along **one axis at a time**.

**The physical image.** A topographic map of a hill. Your altitude `f(x, y)` depends on where you are вҖ” east-west (`x`) and north-south (`y`). You stand at one point. Someone asks:

> "If you take a tiny step **east**, how much does your altitude change?"

That rate is `вҲӮf/вҲӮx` вҖ” the partial derivative with respect to `x`. Your north-south position `y` is frozen during the step. You're only looking at the east-west slope.

> "What if you step **north** instead?"

That rate is `вҲӮf/вҲӮy` вҖ” east-west `x` frozen, north-south slope read off.

Each partial derivative is a **1D slope along one axis**, measured with all other variables held still.

---

### How to compute `вҲӮf/вҲӮx`

Treat every variable *except* `x` as a constant, then differentiate normally with respect to `x`.

That's it. The rules you already know (sum, product, chain) all apply identically вҖ” you just pretend the other variables are fixed numbers for that moment.

---

### Why not a single slope?

Because on a 2D landscape, **different directions have different slopes**. Walking east from a ridge might climb steeply; walking north along the ridge might be flat. The two numbers `вҲӮf/вҲӮx` and `вҲӮf/вҲӮy` capture the slopes of the two standard directions. Other directions are combinations of these two вҖ” see the gradient in [`3.9-gradient-hessian.md`](./3.9-gradient-hessian.md).

---

### Generalizes to any number of variables

`f(xвӮҒ, xвӮӮ, вҖҰ, xвӮҷ)` gives `n` partial derivatives `вҲӮf/вҲӮxвӮҒ, вҲӮf/вҲӮxвӮӮ, вҖҰ`. For each one, freeze every variable except that one, then differentiate. The concept doesn't change вҖ” you're just adding more axes to the landscape.

---

### Where partial derivatives show up

| Domain | Partial derivative role |
|---|---|
| **Gradient descent / backprop** | `вҲӮL/вҲӮw` tells you how the loss changes if you tweak weight `w` alone |
| **Physics of fields** | `вҲӮT/вҲӮx` = how temperature changes per meter in the x-direction |
| **Thermodynamics** | `вҲӮP/вҲӮV` = how pressure changes with volume at fixed temperature |
| **Robotics (Jacobian)** | each entry is `вҲӮ(end-effector coord) / вҲӮ(joint angle)` |

---

**What to remember.**

A partial derivative is a **one-axis slope** on a multi-axis landscape. Freeze every variable except the one you're varying, then differentiate normally. You get one number per axis вҖ” and together they form the **gradient**, which encodes the full local behavior of the landscape.
