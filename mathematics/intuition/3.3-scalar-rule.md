# 3.3 — The Scalar Rule: Constants Just Ride Along

Pure-intuition companion to §3.3 of [`../03-derivatives.md`](../03-derivatives.md). No formulas, no exercises.

---

A constant number in front of a function doesn't interact with `x` at all. When you differentiate, it just sits there and gets carried along.

**The physical image.** You're watching a recorded video of a car trip. The driver's speed, acceleration, heart rate — every rate the car produces — is recorded. Now you press the **2× playback** button. Every rate on the screen is doubled. The car itself didn't change its behavior — it's the *playback* that rescales everything uniformly.

Multiplying a function by a constant `k` is exactly the same as changing the playback speed. Every derivative scales by the same factor `k`. Nothing else moves.

```
       Original                Scaled by k = 3
       f(x)                    3 · f(x)
       ╱                        ╱
      ╱                        ╱
     ╱                        ╱
    ╱                        ╱
   ╱                        ╱
  ─────────── x            ─────────── x
  slope at a = 2           slope at a = 6  (3 × 2)
```

### Why it works

The constant `k` has no dependence on `x` — it doesn't shift, doesn't stretch, doesn't bend when `x` changes. So when you ask "how much does `k · f(x)` change when `x` moves by `dx`?", the answer is just `k` times however much `f` would have changed alone. `k` simply rides on top.

---

**What to remember.**

A constant in front of a function is a **playback-speed knob**. It rescales every rate uniformly, without mixing with anything else. Pull it out, differentiate the rest, put it back in.
