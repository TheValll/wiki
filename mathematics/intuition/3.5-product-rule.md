# 3.5 — The Product Rule: The Growing Rectangle

Pure-intuition companion to §3.5 of [`../03-derivatives.md`](../03-derivatives.md). No formulas, no exercises.

---

The derivative of a product is *not* the product of the derivatives. If two things grow at the same time, both contribute to the area they enclose — and the rule has to account for that. The product rule is what keeps track.

**The physical image.** A rectangular garden where **both the length and the width grow** over time. The length is `u(t)`. The width is `v(t)`. Together they enclose an area `u · v`. How fast does that area grow?

To see it, watch what happens between time `t` and time `t + dt` — a tiny moment later.

---

### Step 1 — the rectangle at time `t`

```
      ┌──────────────────────┐
      │                      │
      │                      │
      │        u · v         │     ← area at time t
      │                      │
      │                      │
      └──────────────────────┘
               u
                                   (height = v)
```

---

### Step 2 — both sides grow a tiny bit

Length grows from `u` to `u + du`. Width grows from `v` to `v + dv`.

```
      ┌──────────────────────┬────┐
      │                      │    │
      │                      │    │
      │        u · v         │ (A)│     ← original area
      │                      │    │
      │                      │    │
      ├──────────────────────┼────┤
      │           (B)        │(C) │     ← new strip on top
      └──────────────────────┴────┘
               u               du

      (A) = strip on the right (width grew)
      (B) = strip on the top   (length grew)
      (C) = tiny corner square (both grew at once)
```

Three new pieces are added:

| Piece | Dimensions | Size |
|---|---|---|
| **(A)** strip on the right | `v × du` | proportional to one tiny quantity |
| **(B)** strip on the top | `u × dv` | proportional to one tiny quantity |
| **(C)** corner square | `du × dv` | proportional to **two** tiny quantities → negligible |

---

### Step 3 — throw away the corner

The corner `(C)` is "twice tiny" — a product of two infinitesimals. Compared to the two strips, it vanishes. Only `(A)` and `(B)` count.

```
      growth of area  =  v · du  +  u · dv
                         ↑            ↑
                     right strip    top strip
```

---

### Step 4 — divide by `dt` to get the rate

The **rate** of area growth is:

```
      (area grown) / dt  =  v · (du/dt)  +  u · (dv/dt)
                          =  u' · v  +  u · v'
```

And that is the product rule. The two pieces — `u'·v` and `u·v'` — are the two strips. The "one grew while the other stayed still" contribution, from each side.

---

### Why you can't just multiply the derivatives

If you naively wrote `(u · v)' = u' · v'`, you'd only be counting how fast the *interaction* between growth rates compounds — and you'd miss both **strips**. That's the entire surface of growth! Each strip is where *one side moves while the other stays in place*, and they are the dominant pieces of the area change.

---

### The quotient rule comes from the same idea

`(u / v)' = (u'·v − u·v') / v²` is the product rule applied carefully to `u · (1/v)`. Same logic — both `u` and `1/v` grow — with a minus sign appearing because `1/v` drops when `v` rises.

---

**What to remember.**

The product rule is a story about a **rectangle growing on two sides at once**. The total area grows by two strips — one from each side expanding while the other stays put. The `u'·v + u·v'` form is simply the sum of those two strips, divided by `dt`.
