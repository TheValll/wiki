# 2.1 — The Discriminant: Does the Parabola Cross the Ground?

Pure-intuition companion to §2.1 of [`../02-algebra-solving.md`](../02-algebra-solving.md). No formulas, no exercises.

---

A quadratic equation asks: where does the parabola cross the x-axis? The discriminant answers this **before** you compute anything — it tells you how many crossings exist, just from the shape.

**The physical image.** Picture a ball-shaped curve: a valley (opens upward) or a hill (opens downward). The x-axis is the ground. The only question is: does the curve *touch the ground*?

There are three — and only three — possibilities.

---

### Case 1 — The valley dips below the ground (two real roots)

The curve enters the ground on the way down, and exits on the way back up.

```
     ╲                           ╱
      ╲                         ╱
       ╲                       ╱
    ────•─────── ground ───────•────
         ╲                    ╱
          ╲__________________╱
          ↑                  ↑
       root 1             root 2
```

Two crossings. Two real roots, distinct.

---

### Case 2 — The valley just kisses the ground (one double root)

The lowest point of the valley sits *exactly* on the x-axis. The curve touches, but does not cross.

```
      ╲                       ╱
       ╲                     ╱
        ╲___________________╱
     ─────────── • ────────── ground
                 ↑
            double root
```

One crossing. The two roots have collapsed into one.

---

### Case 3 — The valley floats entirely above the ground (no real root)

The lowest point of the curve is above the x-axis. The curve never touches it.

```
       ╲                     ╱
        ╲___________________╱


     ────────── ground ─────────────
```

No crossings. No real roots. (They exist in the complex world, but not here.)

---

### What the discriminant actually measures

The vertex of the parabola is its lowest point (for a valley-shaped curve). The two roots, *if they exist*, sit **symmetrically on either side of the vertex**. The discriminant encodes the squared distance between these roots — so:

- **positive** → real separation exists → two distinct roots
- **zero** → the roots collapse into one → the vertex is *on* the ground
- **negative** → "squared distance" would be negative, which is impossible in the real world → no real roots

The sign of the discriminant is a **yes/no answer** available for free, just from the coefficients, without any root-finding.

---

### The three cases in one table

| Discriminant sign | Geometry of the parabola | Real roots |
|---|---|---|
| **positive** | crosses the x-axis twice | two, distinct |
| **zero** | tangent to the x-axis (kisses it once) | one, double |
| **negative** | floats entirely above or below the x-axis | none |

---

**What to remember.**

The discriminant is a **weather forecast for a quadratic**: before you solve anything, it tells you whether the parabola will cross the ground, graze it, or miss it entirely. Its *sign* is the whole answer — the *exact value* only matters when you actually compute the roots.
