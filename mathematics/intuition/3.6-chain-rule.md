# 3.6 — The Chain Rule: Meshed Gears Multiply Rates

Pure-intuition companion to §3.6 of [`../03-derivatives.md`](../03-derivatives.md). No formulas, no exercises.

---

The chain rule is what happens when one function feeds its output into another. The two functions form a pipeline, and their rates of change **multiply**.

**The physical image.** Two **meshed gears**. Gear A is driven by a motor and spins at 3 turns per second. Gear B is chained to A, and for every full turn of A, gear B makes 2 turns. Then gear B spins at `3 × 2 = 6` turns per second.

```
                    ┌───────────────────────────────┐
                    │                               │
   motor            │        gear A       gear B    │        final output
   ───>  3 turns/s ─┼──>  ⭒ ───── ⭒ ────────>      │──>  6 turns/s
                    │     rate          rate        │
                    │     du/dx = 3     df/du = 2   │
                    └───────────────────────────────┘
                              ↑ chained gears

                     total rate = df/du · du/dx = 2 · 3 = 6
```

When two rates are chained, you **multiply** them to get the total rate. That's all the chain rule says.

---

### The three-layer pipeline

A composition `f(g(h(x)))` is a pipeline:

```
        x  ─[ h ]─>  u  ─[ g ]─>  v  ─[ f ]─>  f(x)

      rate of u vs x:   du/dx     (gear h spins this fast per unit x)
      rate of v vs u:   dv/du     (gear g spins this fast per unit u)
      rate of f vs v:   df/dv     (gear f spins this fast per unit v)

      total rate df/dx = df/dv · dv/du · du/dx
```

Every stage contributes one factor. The chain rule generalizes to any length of pipeline — just multiply the rates of each stage.

---

### Why Leibniz notation makes it obvious

In Leibniz form:

```
       df     df     du
      ───  =  ──  ·  ──
       dx     du     dx
```

The `du`'s look like they "cancel" visually. They don't, technically — those aren't real fractions — but the *pattern* is exact, and it's a free mnemonic. See [`3.1-lagrange-vs-leibniz.md`](./3.1-lagrange-vs-leibniz.md).

---

### The "keep the inside" trick

For quick use on simple compositions like `f(x) = (2x + 3)⁵` or `sin(x² + 1)`:

> "Derivative of the **outer** function, keep the **inside** unchanged, times the derivative of the **inside**."

That is just the chain rule with two layers — outer rate, times inner rate.

```
       (2x + 3)⁵           outer: power 5        inner: 2x + 3
                           outer rate: 5·(·)⁴    inner rate: 2
                           → derivative = 5·(2x + 3)⁴ · 2
                                          └────────┘  └─┘
                                          keep inside × inner rate
```

---

### The physical reason the rates multiply

If a small bump in `x` causes `u` to move by a factor `du/dx`, and then that bump in `u` causes `f` to move by a factor `df/du`, the bump in `x` has been amplified **twice**. Once at each stage. The total amplification is the product of the two amplifications. Nothing deeper is going on — it's what happens any time a small signal passes through a chain of linear amplifiers.

---

**What to remember.**

The chain rule is the **gear-ratio rule** for pipelines of functions. When two (or more) functions are chained, their rates **multiply**. The outer function's rate is evaluated at the current value of the inner function, then scaled by the inner function's rate. Every stage of the pipeline contributes one factor.
