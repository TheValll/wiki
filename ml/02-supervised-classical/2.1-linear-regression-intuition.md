# 2.1 — Linear Regression — The Story

> Companion to [`2.1-linear-regression.md`](./2.1-linear-regression.md). No formulas, no exercises — just the story. Read on a train, in bed, between sets.

A continuous thread, told through one little character (Lily and her lemonade stand). Each chapter adds **one detail** to the same picture. By the end, every word in the technical page should map back to something here.

---

## 1 — Lily and the lemonade stand

Lily is 8. Every Saturday, she opens a small lemonade stand in her garden.

Some Saturdays she sells a lot. Other Saturdays, almost nothing. She wants to understand why — and, even better, **predict** how many cups she'll sell next Saturday so she doesn't waste lemons.

```
   Lily's stand
   ┌──────────┐
   │ LEMONADE │
   │   1 €    │
   │   ◯ ◯ ◯  │
   └──┬────┬──┘
      │    │
```

---

## 2 — The notebook

Lily starts a notebook. Every Saturday she writes two things: how hot it was, and how many cups she sold.

```
   Saturday 1   |  30 °C  hot      |  sold 5
   Saturday 2   |  22 °C  warm     |  sold 3
   Saturday 3   |  15 °C  cool     |  sold 2
   Saturday 4   |  35 °C  very hot |  sold 6
   Saturday 5   |  18 °C  cool     |  sold 2
   Saturday 6   |  27 °C  warm     |  sold 4
   Saturday 7   |  12 °C  cold     |  sold 1
   Saturday 8   |  32 °C  hot      |  sold 5
```

Eight Saturdays. Eight little lines. Now what?

---

## 3 — The cloud of dots

Lily draws her notebook on a chart. Temperature on the bottom, cups on the side. Each Saturday becomes a dot.

```
   cups
     │
   6 │                          ●
     │
   5 │                  ●           ●
     │
   4 │              ●
     │
   3 │       ●
     │
   2 │   ●     ●
     │
   1 │ ●
     │
     └────────────────────────────────► temperature (°C)
       10   15   20   25   30   35
```

She squints. The dots **go up**. When it's hot, she sells more. When it's cool, she sells less. Not perfectly — sometimes a 27°C Saturday beats a 30°C one — but the trend is clear.

---

## 4 — Drawing a line

Lily wants a single line that follows the cloud. She takes her ruler.

**First try.** She draws a line too high.

```
   cups
     │
   8 │── ── ── ── ── ── ── ── ── ──   ← her first line
     │
   6 │                          ●
   5 │                  ●           ●
   4 │              ●
   3 │       ●
   2 │   ●     ●
   1 │ ●
     │
     └────────────────────────────────► °C
```

Bad. All her dots are *under* the line. The line is wrong.

**Second try.** She tilts it so it cuts through the middle of the cloud.

```
   cups
     │                            __
     │                       __──
   5 │                  ●─        ●
     │              ●__
   4 │          __──
     │       ●
   3 │  __──     ●
     │
   2 │ ●
   1 │ ●
     │
     └────────────────────────────────► °C
```

Better. Some dots are above the line, some below — that's normal. But how does she know **this** line is the *best* one, and not one slightly more tilted, or slightly higher?

---

## 5 — The rubber bands

Here's the trick. Lily imagines a **rubber band** stretched between each dot and the line, pulling straight up or straight down.

```
   cups
     │                            __
     │                       __──
     │                  ●─        |
     │              ●            ●
     │          __──             ↑
     │       ● ↑                 (rubber band
     │   __──  (stretches up)     stretches down)
     │ ●
     │ ●
     │
     └────────────────────────────────► °C
```

If the dot is **above** the line, its band pulls *up*. If it's **below**, it pulls *down*.

Each rubber band has some stretch — long if the dot is far from the line, short if it's close.

The **best line** is the one where the **total stretch is as small as possible**. No matter how Lily rotates or slides her ruler, she can't make the bands shorter overall.

That's the line her ruler will settle into when she stops fighting it. The line is **balanced**.

---

## 6 — Why squared, not just total

A small but important detail.

Lily doesn't add up the raw stretches. She adds up the **squared** stretches.

Why? Because a rubber band stretched **4 cm** doesn't pull twice as hard as one stretched **2 cm** — it pulls **four times** as hard.

```
   2 cm stretch  →  pull = 4
   4 cm stretch  →  pull = 16   (not 8!)
```

This means **big stretches dominate**. The line spends most of its energy minimising the *biggest* stretches first. Tiny ones barely matter.

The pretty side-effect: when one big stretch shrinks, the line moves a lot. When small stretches change, it barely budges.

---

## 7 — What the line is telling Lily

Lily looks at her balanced line. It has **two settings**:

```
   cups
     │                            __
     │                       __──
     │                  __──            ← steepness:
     │             __──                    "how much more
     │        __──                          per +1°C ?"
     │   __──                            ←  (slope)
     │ ↑
     │ │  ← starting point:
     │ │     "where the line meets
     │ │      the bottom-left edge"
     │ │     (intercept)
     │
     └────────────────────────────────► °C
```

- **Steepness** — how much more she sells for each extra °C. Lily reads: "for every +5°C, I sell about +1 cup".
- **Starting point** — where the line crosses the y-axis. Lily reads: "if it were 0°C, my line predicts 0 cups". Sounds about right — no one wants lemonade in the snow.

Now she can predict **before Saturday morning**. The weather says 28°C? She reads from the line at x = 28: about **4.5 cups**. She makes 4 or 5 cups of lemonade. Probably right.

---

## 8 — The danger zone (predicting outside what we've seen)

The weather says 45°C tomorrow. Lily reads her line at x = 45: it says **9 cups**.

But — Lily has never seen 45°C. Her notebook only covers 12°C to 35°C. **Outside that range**, her line is just guessing.

Maybe at 45°C, people **stay inside** because of the heat. Maybe she sells 0. Maybe 12. Her line cannot know.

```
   cups
     │                                       ?
     │                                    ?
   9 │  - - - - - - - - - - - - - - ●←(line says 9)
     │                                /
     │                          ●____/
     │                  ●_____/
     │              ●______/
     │       ● ●_____/
     │ ●____/
     │
     └─────┬─────────────────────┬─────────────────► °C
          12                    35       45
        (data starts)        (data ends)  (extrapolation)
```

The lesson: a line is only **trustworthy inside the range** it has seen. Outside, it's a brave but uninformed guess.

---

## 9 — The crazy Saturday

One Saturday, a fair sets up in the park next door. 50 children spill into Lily's street. She sells **30 cups**.

```
   cups
  30 │                                        ●  ← crazy Saturday
     │
   ⋮
     │
   6 │                          ●
   5 │                  ●           ●
   4 │              ●
   3 │       ●
   2 │   ●     ●
   1 │ ●
     │
     └────────────────────────────────► °C
```

Should this dot be in her notebook?

If she keeps it, her ruler **bends to chase it**. The line tips up dramatically. Now every future prediction is too high — because the line is bent by one weird day that won't happen again.

```
                                    ___________●  ← line bent
                                  ●            up by the
                                  ←─── normal Saturdays  outlier
                            ●  └─── now under-fit
                       ●
                    ●
                ●  ●
                ● ●
```

That single dot is called an **outlier**. The line treats it like a normal Saturday because the rubber-band rule doesn't know about fairs. Lily decides: she'll **note it down separately** but exclude it from the line.

This is the price of the squared-stretch rule: outliers are loud.

---

## 10 — More than one clue

Lily realises temperature isn't the only thing that matters.

- Some Saturdays, friends come visit → she sells more
- Some Saturdays, kids are playing in the park → she sells more
- Some Saturdays, it rains → she sells nothing

Her notebook now has **four columns**: temperature, friends, kids-in-park, rain-yes-no, sold.

```
   Sat | °C  | friends | kids | rain | sold
   ────┼─────┼─────────┼──────┼──────┼─────
    1  | 30  |    2    |  10  |  no  |  5
    2  | 22  |    0    |   3  |  no  |  3
    3  | 15  |    0    |   1  |  yes |  0
    4  | 35  |    3    |  15  |  no  |  6
   ...
```

The "line" can't be drawn on paper anymore — it lives in 4 dimensions. But the **rule is the same**: rubber bands, squared, balanced.

The shape of the answer is now: *"each clue tilts the prediction in its own way"*.
- +1 °C → +0.2 cups
- +1 friend → +0.5 cups
- +1 kid in park → +0.1 cups
- rain (yes vs no) → −2 cups

Lily multiplies and adds. She gets her prediction for tomorrow.

That's the multivariate version. Same picture, more axes.

---

## 11 — The honest test

Lily's mum asks: *"How do you know your line is any good?"*

Lily realises: if she **fits** the line on a notebook entry, then **checks** the line on the same entry, of course it looks good. That's like grading your own homework using the same homework.

So she does this:

1. **Hide** Saturday 8 from her notebook.
2. **Fit** the line using only Saturdays 1-7.
3. **Predict** Saturday 8 from the line — without looking at the real number.
4. **Compare** her prediction to the real number.

```
   Saturdays 1-7   →  ruler fits the line
                              │
                              ▼
                       prediction for Sat 8: 4.8 cups
                              │
                              ▼
                       reveal Sat 8: 5 cups
                              │
                              ▼
                       error: 0.2 cups   →   pretty good!
```

If the error is small → her line really *learned* something.
If the error is huge → her line just memorised noise.

This is the difference between **fitting** and **predicting**. The big lesson of the whole field.

---

## 12 — The picture to remember

Close your eyes and picture this:

```
   ●        ●
        ●
              ●     ●     ●
       ●               ●
            ●     ●         ●

       a cloud of dots ─── a ruler ─── rubber bands ─── balance
```

- **A cloud of dots** — the data
- **A ruler** that wants to follow the cloud — the line
- **Rubber bands** between the ruler and each dot, pulling vertically — the residuals
- **Balance** — the ruler stops where no rotation makes the bands shorter (in squared total)

Everything else — the formulas, the code, the variants (Ridge, Lasso), the diagnostic plots — is just **details on top of this picture**. When you forget what some technical word means, come back here. The picture explains it.
