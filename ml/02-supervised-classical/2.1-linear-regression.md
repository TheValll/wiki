# 2.1 — Linear Regression

**What it does:** Fits a straight line (or hyperplane) through a cloud of labeled points so future inputs can be predicted by reading off the line. The simplest supervised model and the foundation that every later model either extends, squashes, or replaces.

**Family:** Supervised — Regression (linear, parametric)

**Source:** Deisenroth ch 9 · Hastie & Tibshirani (ESL) ch 3 · Géron ch 4

| Input | Output | Trains via | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vector `x ∈ ℝᵈ` (continuous or one-hot features) | Scalar `ŷ ∈ ℝ` | OLS closed-form **or** gradient descent | Mean Squared Error |

> **How to read this page.** A continuous fil rouge follows Lily and her lemonade stand. Each chapter shows one piece of the model — first as a story moment, then immediately named in ML vocabulary with the formula, link to math wiki, and the mental hook to keep. By chapter 13 the entire model is in your head. The **Practical reference** at the end carries the dense tables (diagnostics, variants, code).

## 1 — Lily and the lemonade stand

Lily is 8. Every Saturday, she opens a small lemonade stand in her garden. Some Saturdays she sells a lot. Other Saturdays, almost nothing. She wants to **predict** how many cups she'll sell next Saturday so she doesn't waste lemons.

```
   Lily's stand
   ┌──────────┐
   │ LEMONADE │
   │   1 €    │
   │   ◯ ◯ ◯  │
   └──┬────┬──┘
```

This is a **regression problem**: predict a continuous number (cups sold) from input features (temperature, etc.). When the underlying relationship is roughly linear, **linear regression** is the simplest and strongest baseline — the first model anyone should reach for.

---

## 2 — The notebook

Lily writes down what happens every Saturday: how hot it was, and how many cups she sold.

```
   Saturday 1   |  30 °C  hot      |  sold 5
   Saturday 2   |  22 °C  warm     |  sold 3
   Saturday 3   |  15 °C  cool     |  sold 2
   Saturday 4   |  35 °C  very hot |  sold 6
   Saturday 5   |  18 °C  cool     |  sold 2
   Saturday 6   |  27 °C  warm     |  sold 4
   Saturday 7   |  12 °C  cold     |  sold 1
   Saturday 8   |  32 °C  hot      |  sold 5
```

The notebook is the **training set**. Each row is a **labeled example** `(xᵢ, yᵢ)`:
- `xᵢ` — the inputs (temperature for now)
- `yᵢ` — the target (cups sold)
- `i` runs from 1 to `n` (here `n = 8` Saturdays)

A model that *learns* from this data will eventually predict `y` for new `x` it has never seen.

---

## 3 — The cloud of dots

Lily plots her notebook on a chart. Temperature on the bottom, cups on the side. Each Saturday becomes a dot.

```
   cups
     │
   6 │                          ●
   5 │                  ●           ●
   4 │              ●
   3 │       ●
   2 │   ●     ●
   1 │ ●
     │
     └────────────────────────────────► temperature (°C)
       10   15   20   25   30   35
```

This is a **scatter plot**. The visible upward trend tells Lily her input and output are **positively correlated** — a useful sanity check before fitting any model. If the cloud were a flat blob with no direction, no line could capture anything and we'd need a different approach altogether.

---

## 4 — Drawing a line

Lily wants a single line that follows the cloud. Her first try is too high — every dot sits below it. Her second try, tilted, cuts through the middle:

```
   cups                          __
     │                       __──
   5 │                  ●─        ●
     │              ●__
   4 │          __──
     │       ●
   3 │  __──     ●
   2 │ ●
   1 │ ●
     │
     └────────────────────────────────► °C
```

That line is the **model**:

```
   ŷ = w · x + b
```

- `ŷ` (read "y-hat") — the prediction the line gives at temperature `x`
- `w` — the slope (steepness)
- `b` — the intercept (where the line crosses the y-axis at `x = 0`)

These two numbers `(w, b)` are the **parameters** of the model. **Training** means finding the best `(w, b)`. But what does "best" mean? — chapter 5.

---

## 5 — The rubber bands

How does Lily know one line is better than another? She imagines a **rubber band** stretched between each dot and the line, pulling vertically.

```
   cups                          __
     │                       __──
     │                  ●─        |
     │              ●            ●
     │          __──             ↑
     │       ● ↑                 (band stretches down)
     │   __──  (stretches up)
     │ ●
     │ ●
     │
     └────────────────────────────────► °C
```

The band pulls **up** if the dot is above the line, **down** if below. Long band when the dot is far away, short when close.

Each rubber band is a **residual** — the signed vertical gap between truth and prediction:

```
   rᵢ = yᵢ − ŷᵢ
       ↑    ↑
     truth  prediction
```

The line Lily wants is the one where the **total stretch is as small as possible**. No matter how she rotates or slides the ruler, she can't make the bands shorter overall. That balanced line is the **best fit**.

---

## 6 — Why squared, not just total

A small but load-bearing detail.

Lily doesn't add up the raw stretches — she adds up the **squared** stretches. Why? Because a rubber band stretched 4 cm doesn't pull twice as hard as one stretched 2 cm — it pulls **four times** as hard.

```
   2 cm stretch  →  squared = 4
   4 cm stretch  →  squared = 16   (not 8!)
```

This means **big stretches dominate**. The line spends most of its energy minimising the largest residuals first. Tiny ones barely matter.

The loss function Lily is minimising is the **Mean Squared Error**:

```
   L(w, b) = (1/n) · Σᵢ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²
           = (1/n) · Σᵢ (yᵢ − (w·xᵢ + b))²
```

**Why squared and not absolute value?** Two reasons. First, squared loss is differentiable everywhere — clean optimisation. Second, under the assumption of Gaussian noise on `y`, minimising MSE is equivalent to **maximum likelihood estimation**. Squared error is the natural choice, not arbitrary.

## 7 — Two settings: slope and intercept

Lily looks at her balanced line. It has exactly two settings:

```
   cups
     │                            __
     │                       __──        ← slope w:
     │                  __──                 "how much more
     │             __──                       per +1 °C ?"
     │        __──
     │   __──
     │ ↑
     │ │  ← intercept b:
     │ │     "where the line meets x = 0"
     │
     └────────────────────────────────► °C
```

- **Slope `w`** — change in `y` per unit change in `x`. Reads as *"for every +1 °C, sales increase by w cups."*
- **Intercept `b`** — value of `y` when `x = 0`. Reads as *"if it were 0 °C, the line predicts b cups."*

This is one of linear regression's superpowers: **interpretability**. Each parameter has a direct real-world meaning. A non-ML person can read the coefficients and understand the model.

---

## 8 — Finding the best ruler

How does Lily *actually* compute the best `(w, b)`? Two paths reach the same answer.

### Path A — Closed form (OLS, normal equations)

Set every partial derivative of the loss to zero, solve the resulting linear system. For the multivariate case with design matrix `X ∈ ℝⁿˣᵈ` and target `y ∈ ℝⁿ`:

```
   ŵ = (XᵀX)⁻¹ Xᵀy
```

**Pros:** exact, one shot, no hyperparameters.
**Cons:** the inversion is `O(d³)` — painful past `d ≈ 10⁴`. Fails outright if `XᵀX` is singular (perfect multicollinearity). In practice we solve via QR or SVD, never by literally inverting.

### Path B — Gradient descent

Walk downhill on the loss surface in small steps:

```
   init  w ← 0,  b ← 0
   loop  ŷ ← w·x + b
         r ← y − ŷ
         w ← w + α · (2/n) · Σᵢ xᵢ · rᵢ
         b ← b + α · (2/n) · Σᵢ        rᵢ
   until convergence
```

**Pros:** scales to huge `d`, streaming data, drops straight into SGD / mini-batch variants — same plumbing as neural nets.
**Cons:** needs learning-rate tuning, no principled stopping rule beyond "loss plateaued".

| Situation | Path |
|---|---|
| `d` ≤ a few thousand, dense | A (closed form via QR) |
| `d` huge, sparse, or streaming | B (SGD) |
| Will later swap to neural net | B (same code path) |

**Geometric reading:** the OLS solution is the **orthogonal projection** of `y` onto the column space of `X`. Every other linear combination of features sits further from `y` in Euclidean distance. There is no "even better" line.

> See also: [`mathematics/04-optimization/4.1-gradient-descent.md`](../../mathematics/04-optimization/4.1-gradient-descent.md), [`mathematics/01-linear-algebra/1.5-inverse.md`](../../mathematics/01-linear-algebra/1.5-inverse.md) (when `XᵀX` is invertible).

## 9 — The danger zone

The weather says 45 °C tomorrow. Lily reads her line at `x = 45`: it says **9 cups**.

But Lily's notebook only covers 12 °C to 35 °C. **Outside that range**, her line is just guessing.

```
   cups                                    ?
     │                                  ?
   9 │  - - - - - - - - - - - - - - ●←(line says 9)
     │                          ●____/
     │                  ●_____/
     │              ●______/
     │       ● ●_____/
     │ ●____/
     │
     └─────┬─────────────────────┬─────────────────► °C
          12                    35       45
        (data starts)        (data ends)  (extrapolation)
```

This failure is called **extrapolation**. The model is **only trustworthy inside the range of the training data**. Outside, the line keeps a straight slope, but the world doesn't have to. People might stay inside at 45 °C; the line cannot know.

Always check the **range of each feature** in your training set, and flag predictions outside it.

## 10 — The crazy Saturday

One Saturday, a fair sets up next door. Lily sells **30 cups** instead of 4.

```
   cups                                 ●  ← crazy Saturday
  30 │                                  
     │
   ⋮
     │
   6 │                          ●
   5 │                  ●
     │              ●
   4 │       ●
     │   ●     ●
   1 │ ●
     │
     └────────────────────────────────► °C
```

If Lily keeps that point, MSE squares its residual — the loss is dominated by this one Saturday. Her ruler **bends to chase it**, ruining predictions on normal Saturdays.

This single point is an **outlier**. It's the price of the squared-residual rule: **low resistance to outliers**. Three counter-measures:

| Counter-measure | When |
|---|---|
| Identify and exclude | Outliers are obvious data errors or one-off events |
| Use a **robust loss** (Huber: squared near 0, linear far away) | Outliers are rare but real |
| Use **RANSAC** (fit on random subsets, keep the line that fits the majority) | Many outliers expected |

---

## 11 — More than one clue

Lily realises temperature isn't the only thing that matters. She also tracks: friends visiting, kids in the park, whether it rained. Her notebook now has multiple input columns:

```
   Sat | °C  | friends | kids | rain | sold
   ────┼─────┼─────────┼──────┼──────┼─────
    1  | 30  |    2    |  10  |  no  |  5
    2  | 22  |    0    |   3  |  no  |  3
    3  | 15  |    0    |   1  |  yes |  0
   ...
```

The model becomes a sum over features:

```
   ŷ = b + w₁·x₁ + w₂·x₂ + … + wₐ·xₐ
      = b + wᵀx
```

In matrix form, for the whole training set at once:

```
       X (n × d)         w (d)         ŷ (n)
       ┌──────────────┐  ┌────┐       ┌────┐
       │ x₁¹  …  x₁ᵈ  │  │ w₁ │       │ ŷ₁ │
       │ x₂¹  …  x₂ᵈ  │  │ w₂ │ + b = │ ŷ₂ │
       │  ⋮             │  │ ⋮  │       │ ⋮  │
       │ xₙ¹  …  xₙᵈ  │  │ wₐ │       │ ŷₙ │
       └──────────────┘  └────┘       └────┘
```

The "line" is now a **tilted hyperplane** through the cloud. Same rubber-band rule, same MSE loss, same OLS or gradient descent. Just more axes.

Each `wⱼ` reads as **"effect of clue `j`, holding the others fixed"**:
- `w₁ = +0.2` cups per °C
- `w₂ = +0.5` cups per friend
- `w_rain = −2` cups when it rains

Lily multiplies, adds, gets her prediction.

## 12 — The honest test

Lily's mum asks: *"How do you know your line is any good?"*

Lily realises: if she fits the line on her notebook, then checks it on the same notebook, she's grading her own homework using the same homework. Useless.

So she does this:

```
   Saturdays 1-7   →  fit the line
                              │
                              ▼
                       prediction for Sat 8 : 4.8
                              │
                              ▼
                       reveal Sat 8 truth   : 5
                              │
                              ▼
                       error                : 0.2  →  good
```

This is the **train / test split**. The training set is what the model fits on. The test set is hidden until the very end and used to measure **generalisation** — does the model work on data it has never seen?

Common metrics on the test set:

| Metric | Range | Read as |
|---|---|---|
| **RMSE** | `[0, ∞)` | Typical prediction error in target units |
| **MAE** | `[0, ∞)` | Median-style error, robust to outliers |
| **R²** | `(-∞, 1]` | Fraction of variance explained — `0` = same as predicting the mean, `<0` = worse |
| **Adjusted R²** | `(-∞, 1]` | R² with a penalty for added features (use to compare models of different `d`) |

Three diagnostic outcomes:

| Train error | Test error | Diagnosis |
|---|---|---|
| Small | Small (close) | Good fit |
| **Small** | **Huge** | **Overfitting** — model memorised noise |
| Huge | Huge | Underfitting — model too simple |

> See also: [`mathematics/05-ml-basics/5.4-rmse.md`](../../mathematics/05-ml-basics/5.4-rmse.md)

## 13 — The picture to remember

Three core mental moves:

1. **Plot the data** → cloud of dots
2. **Draw a line** → make rubber bands → balance them in squared total → that's OLS
3. **Test on data the line has never seen** → that's the only honest measure of quality

```
   ●        ●
        ●
              ●     ●     ●
       ●               ●
            ●     ●         ●

       cloud ─── ruler ─── rubber bands ─── balance ─── honest test
```

That is the whole algorithm. Everything below — diagnostic plots, regularisation, polynomial features, robust losses, the Bayesian view — is a **modification of this exact recipe**, never a replacement. When the next concept feels obscure, come back to Lily.

## Practical reference

The dense tables that didn't fit naturally in the story.

### Diagnostic plots

| Plot | Should look like | Failure pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Residuals vs fitted | Cloud with no structure, mean ≈ 0 | Curve / funnel = non-linearity or heteroscedasticity |
| Q-Q plot of residuals | Straight diagonal line | Tails curving off = non-Gaussian noise (CIs unreliable) |
| Residuals vs each feature | Cloud, no slope | Slope = a feature still carries unmodelled signal |
| Cook's distance | All points small | Spike = a single point is bending the fit (high leverage) |

### Failure modes

| Mode | Signature | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Heteroscedasticity** | Funnel-shaped residual plot | Weighted least squares, log-transform `y`, robust standard errors |
| **Multicollinearity** | Huge VIF (> 10), unstable coefficients | Drop a redundant feature, switch to Ridge |
| **Non-linearity** | Curved residual plot | Polynomial / interaction features, or change model |
| **Outliers** | Huge Cook's distance on 1-2 points | Investigate, use Huber or RANSAC |

### Hyperparameters

Vanilla OLS has **none**. Knobs appear with variants:

| Hyperparameter | Effect | Typical range | Symptom if mistuned |
|---|---|---|---|
| `α` (learning rate, GD only) | Step size | 1e-4 → 1e-1 | Too big: loss diverges. Too small: glacial. |
| `λ` (Ridge / Lasso) | Regularisation strength | 1e-4 → 1e2, log-scaled | Too big: weights crushed to 0. Too small: no effect. |
| Polynomial degree | Capacity of polynomial features | 1 → 5 | Too high: train R² → 1, test R² collapses |
| Feature scaling | Required for GD and any regularised variant | standard or min-max | Without it: dominant-magnitude features eat the gradient |

### Variants

| Variant | Key diff | When to prefer |
|---|---|---|
| **Ridge** | Adds `λ‖w‖²` to loss | Multicollinearity present, want stable weights |
| **Lasso** | Adds `λ‖w‖₁` to loss | Want **feature selection** — drives weights to exactly 0 |
| **ElasticNet** | Mix of L1 + L2 | Mid-ground when correlated feature groups exist |
| **Polynomial regression** | Feeds polynomial features `[x, x², x³, …]` | Mild known non-linearity; still linear in parameters |
| **GLM** (Poisson, Gamma, …) | Different link function + non-Gaussian noise | Counts, rates, strictly positive targets |
| **Bayesian linear regression** | Posterior over `w`, not a point estimate | Need calibrated uncertainty, small data |

### Implementation pointer

```python
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression, Ridge, Lasso
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error, r2_score
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split
import numpy as np

X, y = ...  # X: (n, d), y: (n,)
X_tr, X_te, y_tr, y_te = train_test_split(X, y, test_size=0.2, random_state=0)

model = LinearRegression().fit(X_tr, y_tr)
y_hat = model.predict(X_te)

print(f"weights : {model.coef_}")
print(f"bias    : {model.intercept_:.3f}")
print(f"RMSE    : {np.sqrt(mean_squared_error(y_te, y_hat)):.3f}")
print(f"R²      : {r2_score(y_te, y_hat):.3f}")
```

For Ridge / Lasso swap the constructor and pick `alpha` via `RidgeCV` / `LassoCV` (built-in cross-validation).
